The Indian Goods and Services Tax (GST) system was designed to create a transparent, self-policing tax network. However, it has also given rise to one of the most sophisticated financial crimes of the decade: **Fake Invoicing and Circular Trading**.

In 2025 alone, the Directorate General of GST Intelligence (DGGI) unearthed syndicates that generated thousands of crores in fake Input Tax Credit (ITC) without any actual movement of goods.

If your business unknowingly accepts a fake invoice from a fraudulent vendor and uses that ITC to offset your tax liability, the GST department will treat you as a co-conspirator. You will face blocked ITC ledgers, asset attachment, and severe criminal penalties. 

Here is your 2026 survival guide on how to spot fake invoices and verify vendor authenticity.

## What is a Fake GST Invoice?

A fake invoice is a tax invoice generated by a registered entity where **no actual supply of goods or services has taken place**. 

Fraudsters create shell companies using stolen identities (PAN and Aadhaar cards). They register for GST, generate massive e-invoices billing other companies, and pass on fraudulent ITC. The "buyer" claims this ITC in their GSTR-3B to reduce their tax payout, paying the fraudster a commission in cash. Eventually, the shell company vanishes without ever paying the government the tax it collected.

## 4 Red Flags of a Fake Vendor

Before you sign a large purchase order, look for these warning signs:

### 1. Inconsistent Principal Place of Business
You are buying ₹50 Lakhs worth of industrial steel, but the vendor's registered address on the GST portal points to a small residential apartment or a shared co-working desk. High-volume commodity traders require actual warehouses and godowns. 

### 2. Strange E-Way Bill Patterns
The invoice shows goods moving from Delhi to Mumbai, but no E-Way Bill was generated, or the E-Way Bill shows a vehicle number belonging to a two-wheeler (scooter) transporting 5 tons of cement. The GST portal now flags vehicle mismatches automatically via VAHAN database integration.

### 3. Missing GSTR-3B Filings
The vendor generates an e-Invoice (which flows into your GSTR-2B), but they never file their GSTR-3B to actually pay the tax to the government. If a vendor consistently files GSTR-1 but defaults on GSTR-3B, their GSTIN will eventually be suspended, taking your ITC down with it.

### 4. Bizarre HSN Codes
A company registered with a core business activity of "IT Software Services" is suddenly issuing massive invoices for "Scrap Metal" or "Gold Bullion".

## How to Verify a GST Invoice Instantly

You must integrate a strict vendor KYC (Know Your Customer) process into your accounts payable workflow. Never release payment to a new vendor without performing these checks:

### Step 1: Verify the GSTIN Status
Before processing an invoice, you must ensure the vendor's GSTIN is perfectly active and not suspended or cancelled.

### Step 2: Check the Filing History
A legitimate business files its returns on time. On the GST portal, you can view a vendor's public filing history. If you see that their GSTR-3B has not been filed for the last 3 months, **do not pay the GST component of their invoice**. Withhold the tax amount until they prove they have filed and paid the government.

### Step 3: Scan the E-Invoice QR Code
If the vendor's turnover exceeds ₹5 Crores, they must issue a dynamic e-Invoice with an IRN (Invoice Reference Number) and a QR code. 
Download the official government QR Code verification app and scan the invoice. The app will cryptographically verify if the IRN was actually signed by the Invoice Registration Portal (IRP). If the QR code is unscannable or shows mismatched data, the invoice is forged in Photoshop.
